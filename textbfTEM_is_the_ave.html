TEM is the average monthly temperature (°C/month)
UET is the estimated, unadjusted potential evapotranspiration (mm/month)
ETo is the estimated, adjusted potential evapotranspiration (mm/month).
PRCPT is the average monthly total precipitation (mm/month).
DIFF (PRCPT - ETo) is the rainfall and minus the adjusted potential evapotranspiration (mm/month).
DEF (ETo - PRCPT) is the estimated deficit or unmet atmospheric demand for moisture (mm/month).
5. Discussion
The main objective of this study was to examine climate evolution in Lake Guiers Basin, by applying -using RClimDex program to detect recent trends and AOGCMs through Climate Wizard to predict future climate. Results show that a warming trend is noted (both in past and future projections), with higher maximum air temperature and extremes. Warm days are more (less) frequent. Heat waves, i.e., periods with high maximum air temperatures, are becoming longer.
AOGCMs warming projections may range between 2.57°C - 5.33°C by the end of the century. Although A2 represents an extreme situation relative to the B1 case, it represents a situation in which atmospheric CO2 accumulation continues at gradually accelerating rates over the course of this century. Because Lake Guiers Basin has already experienced a warming of 1.05°C during the last two decades, it is therefore reasonable to conclude that the A2 path might produce an additional warming close to what most models suggest by century’s end.
Furthermore, because we do not yet know how greenhouse gas emissions will change in future, we cannot be sure of exactly how much temperatures will rise, only that the general direction of change will be that of warming throughout the present century.
The model simulations presented in this study nonetheless allow us to estimate the range of annual-scale warming in Lake Guiers Basin that is most reasonable to expect and prepare for.
In terms of precipitations, we detect an increasing PRCPTOT trend on a rate of (+6.17 mm/year) particularly since 2000s. Heavy rainfall events have become stronger, i.e., associated with a higher volume of rainfall each year, or they are becoming more frequent, or both. Does this mean we are at the start of the recovery period?
[9-2] found that it is impossible to answer this question using statistical method, especially trend detection methods. Interpretation of detected linear trends requires long time series data set especially when you are dealing with climate variability. Or, the period covered by the observations is still too short to resolve the question of whether there is now a trend towards wetter conditions. [32], found that there has been some “recovery” in western part of Sahel with respect to the very dry conditions that prevailed during the 30 years 1968–97. However, the recovery was generally weaker in August (which is generally the wettest month) than during the season as a whole.
Climate variability is known to be high in the Sahel, and studies such as that of [9-3] highlight an inconsistent linear trends in Senegalese rainfall indices; [32-2] attributed a large portion of this to multi-decadal sea surface temperature variability and [33] attributed it to the multi-decadal variability of rainfall in the Sahel.
Precipitation is extremely variable in space and time, and future precipitation patterns are therefore more difficult to model and predict than temperature trends. Most, of the AOGCMs predict a significant decrease in annual precipitation in Lake Guiers Basin by century’s end, although the magnitudes of change varied among models and emissions scenarios. This trend is more marked by end century in A1B scenario (-11.56%) and A2 (-15.43%).
Temperature rise will affect the water balance by enhancing potential evapotranspiration (2102.43 mm/year) in the Basin. This effect will be more severe when combined with the decrease of precipitation. In general, the future climate of Lake Guiers Basin seems to be exposed to more severe conditions and water availability will be under much greater stress. Ensemble model projections show by the end of this century -15.43% decrease in precipitation and up to 3.6˚C of temperature rise in A2 scenario
The accuracy of these forecasts is impossible to ascertain in advance because we did not screen models by testing their ability to reconstruct known climatic conditions in the past.
This kind of problem is typical of many such models, which owing to their relatively coarse spatial resolution (order of a few hundred kilometers), are often not suitable for simulating detailed regional weather and climate patterns [34]. Thus a Regional Climate Model (RCM) driven by different AOGCMs is perhaps the most significant current requirement from a hydrological perspective in the climate community [12-2].
We are therefore far less certain of what the future holds for Lake Guiers Basin in terms of precipitation than we are for temperature.
In summary, it seems reasonable to expect total annual precipitation in Lake Guiers Basin to decrease by as much as 2–25% by the end of this century, though the models conflict in those projections more than they do for temperature.
Although total annual precipitation is expected to decrease during this century under an A2 emissions scenario, according to most models, it remains unclear how that decrease will be distributed throughout the year. It is also possible that the average intensity and frequency of heavy precipitation events could increase over time, and that short-term droughts could become more frequent as evaporation rates increase with warming, even if rainfall does not change much [29-2]. This lack of consensus among model projections is unfortunate because the seasonality of precipitation can be even more ecologically important than the total annual amount.
Finally, it is important to consider that all of the climatic trends described in this study will be accompanied by potentially large short-term fluctuations from year to year and decade to decade. It is therefore wiser for water resource manager in Lake Guiers Basin to prepare for a wide range of climatic conditions in the future than to expect a single, smooth pattern of change.
